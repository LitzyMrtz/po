History

The conflict between Gaza and Israel is complex and longstanding, with roots in historical, political, and religious tensions.

The conflict dates back to the late 19th and early 20th centuries with the rise of Jewish nationalism (Zionism) and Arab nationalism in the region. In 1948, Israel declared independence, leading to a war with neighboring Arab states and the displacement of hundreds of thousands of Palestinians, known as the Nakba (catastrophe).

The territory known as Gaza came under Egyptian control, while the West Bank was occupied by Jordan.

Following the 1967 Six-Day War, Israel occupied the West Bank, Gaza Strip, Golan Heights, and Sinai Peninsula. Israeli settlements in the West Bank and East Jerusalem have expanded, leading to ongoing disputes over land and resources.

Gaza came under Israeli occupation until 2005 when Israel withdrew its settlers and military forces. However, Israel still controls Gaza's borders, airspace, and coastline, imposing a blockade that severely restricts the movement of people and goods. Hamas, an Islamist militant group, won legislative elections in Gaza in 2006 and seized control of the territory from the Palestinian Authority in 2007.

Israel, the United States, and the European Union classify Hamas as a terrorist organization due to its attacks against Israeli civilians and refusal to recognize Israel's right to exist. The conflict between Israel and Gaza, often characterized by rocket attacks from Gaza and Israeli airstrikes, has erupted into several major conflicts, notably in 2008-2009, 2012, 2014, and 2021.

The conflicts have resulted in significant casualties, destruction, and humanitarian crises in Gaza, as well as casualties and fear among Israeli civilians. Ceasefire agreements brokered by Egypt, the United Nations, and other mediators have temporarily halted the violence, but underlying issues remain unresolved. The conflict has drawn international condemnation, with calls for a peaceful resolution, respect for human rights, and a two-state solution, whereby Israel and Palestine would coexist side by side. Efforts to broker peace, including negotiations led by the United States, the United Nations, and regional powers, have so far failed to achieve a lasting settlement.
